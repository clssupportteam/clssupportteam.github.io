Offering General Guidance

TUO

When you registered the death, did you use the “Tell us Once” service?

Note - Tailor advice around whether they have registered death yet or not.

"The Tell Us Once Service notifies all government departments (such as DWP, DVLA, Passport, Benefits, Council Tax, HMRC and local Council for Council Tax). We recommend you use this service if you haven’t done so already as it will save you making multiple phone calls."

Car insurance and Tax

Tell Us Once will deal with the government departments. One thing to consider is the DVLA will get notified — is there any vehicle to consider?

Note - Any unused car tax will be refunded

If you are intending to drive the vehicle, you will need to insure and tax the vehicle in your name. You may also want to consider SORNing the vehicle until any decision is made on what to do with it.

Note - If selling, the NOK/Exec can do this and send V5 paperwork to the DVLA Sensitive Case work team, with a copy of the death certificate to process change of ownership.

Home Insurance

May I ask, did your [Insert Relationship] own their property?

Note - It is important to continue to insure and secure the property now, especially if it is unoccupied. We recommend you make contact with the home insurer to discuss ongoing cover.

Council tax

Is anyone living in the property?

[ If Yes ] : If you (or someone else) are now living in the property alone, you may qualify for the sole occupancy discount. Tell Us Once does notify the local council, but it could take several weeks to affect the payments, so you can always contact the local authority directly either online or by phone to get payments reduced sooner.

[ If No ] : It's possible the property could be exempt from council tax for a short period. Please notify the local authority either online or by phone to discuss with them.

Bereavement Support Payment

Note - This is only applicable if there is a surviving spouse/civil partner/partner

Option 1 – Spouse/civil partner/living with a partner who are under state pension age.

There is a possibility that [ you / the spouse/civil partner / the partner ] may qualify for the Bereavement Support Payment. So long as [you / the spouse/civil partner / the partner] are/is under state pension age, and depending on the deceased’s National Insurance contributions, you may be eligible for this payment. This payment is not means tested, and depending on circumstances can be as much as an initial lump sum of £3,500 plus a monthly payment over 18 months.

To ensure you receive the full amount due you MUST apply for it within 3 months of your [insert relationship] passing.

If you have a pen handy, I can give you details of how to check and apply for this - You can apply online via Gov.UK or by calling the bereavement services helpline on 0800 151 2012, or by post (details on UKGOV.com)

Option 2 - Spouse/Civil Partner/partner are over state pension age

You may want to speak to the Department for Work and Pensions about your own situation to see if you qualify for other payments/allowances now the deceased has passed.

ISA Transferable Allowance

Note - This is only applicable if there is a surviving spouse/civil partner/partner

Some tax benefits are transferrable between spouses/civil partners – did the deceased have an ISA either a cash ISA or a Stocks and Shares ISA as part of their savings?

[ If Yes ] : You may want to investigate if you can transfer the ISA allowance. This would allow the saving to stay within its ‘ISA tax wrapper’ and move to [You / the spouse / the civil partner] tax free. To do this, we would suggest you notify the bank of your wish to utilise this option, ideally when notifying them to freeze all the sole accounts.

To help you with this I can arrange an appointment with one of our Probate Consultants and they can talk to you about your situation and what we can do to help.

The meeting with the consultant is without any obligation and they will go through everything with you so you can fully understand what Probate is and what your choices are to administer the estate.
